## Data-layer quality check

The repository map itself is sound on method: it explicitly says to use derived layers for orientation, but to verify important claims against canonical or public sources, and it explicitly warns not to treat generated data-layer files as public policy sources. It also points researchers to the Almere current-view and D6 responsibility-register files as structured bases rather than primary evidence. citeturn47view0

The current D6 register rows for *Inloopvoorzieningen sociaal en gezond* and *Burgerinitiatieven en informele steun* are conservative in a way that the public search supports. Both rows have `owner: null`, `confidence: low`, `decision_status: review_needed`, and explicit notes that local inventory and public local source intake are still needed before partner names or ownership can be treated as facts. That caution should remain. citeturn12view0turn12view1

Where the top-level data layer looks weak is not in being too definite, but in being **too dependent on national-orientation material** and too thin on current local source replacement. The rows currently point back to `data/extracted/d6_governance_collaboration.json`; the wider D6 orientation file itself samples national D6 language and includes an explicit warning that the claims are “orientation only” and that source passages should be used before writing bestuurlijke text. Against that background, the new local public sources found here are a clear upgrade path and the current top layer appears **under-sourced locally**. citeturn12view0turn12view1turn14view1turn47view0

There is also a likely **staleness problem in counts and inventories**. The 2020 welzijnskader says Almere had seventeen buurtcentra, five with a buurtkamer, plus some other meeting places. Current De Schoor pages say the Initiatievenbureau works for eighteen buurtcentra and the live Buurtkamers page lists materially more named buurtkamers than the old policy text. If any top-level layer repeats the older count as a present-tense fact, it should be refreshed or explicitly dated. citeturn35view0turn37view1turn27view0

The top layer should also distinguish more clearly between **four different things that are presently easy to conflate**: municipal support instruments for initiatives and neighbourhood meetings; public access points such as wijkteam inloopspreekuren; partner-run buurtkamers and neighbourhood rooms; and broader prevention/positive-health programmes such as the Almeers Preventieakkoord or Positief Gezond Almere. Public evidence shows all four exist, but they are not the same thing and should not be merged into one implied “D6 owner structure.” citeturn18view6turn25view0turn34view1turn34view2turn18view11turn41view0

Finally, the layer should be careful not to read **public commissioning context as ownership**. The municipal subsidy register shows public relationships with De Schoor, VMCA, Humanitas, neighbourhood-meeting-place recipients and wijkteams-related lines, but that still does not settle which component belongs in the D6 register, who owns it, or how the components should be grouped. citeturn46view0turn46view2turn46view3

## Remaining uncertainty and human validation needed

What still needs human validation is the **formal boundary question**: which of the publicly visible buurtkamers, buurtcentra, inloopspreekuren, wijkteam locations, resident initiatives and informal-support routes should count as part of a formal D6 *sociale en gezonde basisinfrastructuur* row, and which are better understood as adjacent local infrastructure, subsidy instruments or programme activity. The public sources show a lot of activity, but they do not provide a single official Almere boundary document for this row. citeturn35view0turn18view6turn25view0turn34view0turn27view0

Human validation is also still needed on **owner/coordinator questions**. Public pages name the municipality, wijkteams, De Schoor, VMCA, Humanitas, MEE Samen, and in some cases broader programmes such as Positief Gezond Almere, but they do not assign one final accountable owner for the D6 row. Publicly named actor is not the same as formal owner. citeturn34view1turn34view2turn22view0turn39view1turn41view0

The same applies to **commissioning, subsidy and finance interpretation**. The subsidy register shows that the municipality publicly funds or subsidises multiple organisations and neighbourhood-meeting-place activities, but it does not answer how those lines should be grouped for D6, whether a programme is structural or project-based, or what portion of a partner’s public activity is relevant to this specific row. That remains a controller/contract-management and policy-owner question. citeturn45view0turn46view0turn46view2turn46view3

A final uncertainty is **currency and completeness of the live inventory**. Current partner pages are richer than the old repository row, but inventories change. Some municipal area pages also show that residents still experience poor visibility, fragmented information, and differences between neighbourhoods in what exists and what is known. That means any repository inventory should either be dated clearly or validated locally before being treated as a settled map. citeturn32view2turn33view1turn27view0

## Practical implication for Almere

For the Almere D6 review queue, the practical improvement is clear: the row should no longer be treated as if it only has a **generic national D6 basis** plus unnamed local speculation. There is enough public evidence to say that Almere already has a visible ecosystem of resident-initiative support, neighbourhood meeting infrastructure, wijkteam-based walk-in access, volunteer and mantelzorg support, and partner organisations that operate in this space. That should reduce the “named public-source gap” and support better source-backed partner mapping. citeturn18view6turn25view0turn34view1turn34view2turn18view8turn27view0turn37view3turn22view0turn39view1

What can be clarified now is **source hierarchy and wording**. The safest repository wording is that public evidence shows these are **publicly visible actors, access points and support mechanisms in Almere**, and that some prevention/positive-health sources also connect them to wider wellbeing and informal-care agendas. The wording should avoid saying that Almere has formally designated them as the D6 basisinfrastructuur or that one named organisation owns the row. citeturn18view11turn41view0turn47view0turn12view0turn12view1

What must remain human-only is the decision on **formal D6 classification, final owner/coordinator, mandate, structural budget split, and the question whether particular neighbourhood locations or partner programmes should be counted inside or outside the D6 responsibility register**. Public research can now materially strengthen the file, but it still cannot settle the register. citeturn12view0turn12view1turn14view1turn47view0
