# AZWA, IZA and GALA for the Almere case

## Headline conclusions

The core policy logic across AZWA, IZA, and GALA is not simply “more local welfare activity.” It is a structured shift from sickness and late treatment toward health, prevention, and earlier help; from siloed domains toward cooperation between the medical and social domains; and from ad hoc local pilots toward D5 basisfunctionaliteiten and D6 basisinfrastructuur that can be scaled regionally and, eventually, nationally. In other words, the norm is a transition architecture: organize help earlier, closer to residents, with clearer regional-local coordination and with measurable impact on accessibility, workforce pressure, and unnecessary use of insured care. citeturn3view0turn9view3turn5view5turn29view6

For Almere, that architecture already has a concrete local vehicle: the Flevoland IZA regioplan and the Positief Gezond Almere transformation route. Those sources show that Almere is not starting from zero. The city already has a cross-domain collaboration model built around positive health, neighborhood-oriented support, and practical connections between professionals, residents, welfare work, and primary care. What is still unresolved is not the overall direction, but parts of the exact governance, mandate, and financing path through which that direction must now be formalized under AZWA. citeturn6view1turn6view2turn20view0turn14view4

The main bottom line is therefore this: Almere should treat AZWA less as a reason to invent a new local vision, and more as a reason to make its existing health-and-wellbeing coalition operational in the required regional and financial formats. The hardest work is now sequencing: clarifying who approves what, through which regional route, in which budget year, and with which evidence threshold. citeturn7view3turn10view4turn19view0

## Norm

The corpus mixes different kinds of normativity. Some elements are hard-edged process norms. Regional IZA activity is funded through a mandaathouder or mandaatgemeente; that actor applies, accounts, and reports through the SiSa logic; and the regime explicitly guards against double funding with the Broad SPUK. In addition, the minister has explicitly stated that the municipality or mandaatgemeente must give explicit approval to transformation-plan components that concern the social domain, because those components can create structural implementation duties and costs. Those are not merely aspirations; they are approval and accountability conditions. citeturn29view4turn23view0

Other elements are best understood as strong policy expectations rather than direct municipal legal duties. AZWA D6 expects recognizable neighborhood and regional basic infrastructure: low-threshold inloopvoorzieningen, stronger local teams, links to first-line care and public health, support for citizen initiatives, GGD-linked health work such as healthy-school activity, and regional coordination around implementation, monitoring, and digitalization. CW3.1 makes the intent explicit: D5 is meant to reduce inflow into Zvw care by strengthening cooperation between care and social support, while D6 is meant to provide the neighborhood and regional structure that makes that possible. citeturn6view4turn6view5turn5view5

That means the most accurate reading for policymakers is this: the accords do not yet function as a standalone local ordinance telling Almere exactly how to organize every neighborhood function. They function as a binding intergovernmental and inter-organizational framework that becomes operational through regional plans, specific-grant conditions, transformation-plan approval, contractering, and later governance and monitoring documents. Almere therefore cannot treat D5 and D6 as optional, but it also cannot pretend that every local detail is already finally settled. citeturn7view3turn6view3turn29view5

## Time

The time logic is layered. IZA set the regional foundation by requiring regiobeelden and regioplannen, with the regiobeeld route organized at the scale of the zorgkantoorregio and with regional collaboration meant to shape later transformation choices. GALA then overlaid a 2023–2026 municipal funding and planning cycle through the Broad SPUK, requiring a plan of approach for 2024–2026 and building regional prevention infrastructure in parallel. The IZA SPUK itself is multi-year for 2024, 2025, and 2026, with final accountability after the last verantwoordingsinformatie in July 2027. citeturn9view0turn8view1turn29view4

AZWA adds a sharper D5/D6 implementation timetable. The national D5 framework and prioritization route were to be fixed in late 2025; governance for the D5 toolkit was to be worked out in early 2026; a regioscan and practical handreikingen were to support regions in spring 2026; and the regional additional plans for breakthrough deployment are due by the end of September 2026. The agreement also states that basisfunctionaliteiten are to be updated in 2028 on the basis of monitoring and evaluation, while the strategic aim is national coverage of the now-known basisfunctionaliteiten from 2030 onward. citeturn7view3turn7view5turn6view3turn7view4

There is also an important current-status overlay from April 2026. The cabinet’s 9 April 2026 progress letter says the first milestone is the regioscan, says handreikingen will support regions in spring 2026, states that regions then start preparing a work agenda, and notes that an integrated IZA/AZWA monitor will be designed in the second half of 2026. The same letter also shows how crowded the transformation calendar already is: by 6 March 2026, 180 transformation plans had received funding, the transformation budget was exhausted, and 48 plans were on a waiting list. citeturn10view4

For Almere, time therefore matters in two ways. First, the city has to line up its local choices with the 2026 regional-work-agenda window, not after it. Second, it has to fit new structural ambitions into funding and accountability cycles that were largely designed before AZWA was finalized. That makes “budget calendar alignment” a real issue, not a reporting formality. citeturn19view0turn29view4turn10view4

## Money and governance

The national money picture is substantial, but it is not frictionless. AZWA states that for the social domain €371 million is structurally available, plus €196 million incidentally for 2027 and 2028. It also adds €400 million per year in 2027 and 2028 as “doorbraakmiddelen” for impact-heavy transformations, and it explicitly links part of the D6 neighborhood offer to targeted use of €100 million in 2027 and again in 2028 for the social domain. At the same time, the IZA progress letter shows that the broader transformatiemiddelen route is already saturated, which means access to capital is becoming a sequencing and selection problem, not just a design problem. citeturn5view3turn7view1turn7view7turn10view4

Not all of this money is directly reachable by Almere on its own. AZWA states that municipalities cannot act as independent applicants for breakthrough funds; instead, regional or integrated routes are used, with VWS disposing resources regionally or per municipality through a specific grant after assessment. The April 2026 progress letter further indicates that when good plans do not fit within the exhausted transformation envelope, parties may still look for alternatives through contractering, own resources, cooperation with private finance, or by splitting plans into smaller components. That means the real question for Almere is not “is there national money?” but “through which route does a local-regional package become admissible and financeable?” citeturn7view2turn10view3

Governance is correspondingly shared and layered. National coordination for D5 and D6 sits mainly with entity["organization","Ministerie van Volksgezondheid, Welzijn en Sport","netherlands"], entity["organization","Vereniging van Nederlandse Gemeenten","netherlands"], and entity["organization","Zorgverzekeraars Nederland","netherlands"], with decisions on framework and prioritization going through BO IZA/AZWA. Regionally, municipalities and insurers are supposed to sit as equals when regional plans for the total breakthrough package are assessed. Locally, the municipality or mandaatgemeente retains a decisive approval role wherever the social domain creates structural obligations or budget exposure. citeturn7view3turn5view1turn23view0

The most important practical governance insight is therefore simple: ownership is distributed. No single Almere actor “owns” the whole agenda. The city council, the college, the regional municipal route, the insurer side, and provider coalitions each own different approvals, budgets, and execution pieces. Treating D5/D6 as if one local program office can settle everything would misread the corpus. citeturn9view2turn23view0turn29view4

## Locality and execution

The locality question is where the corpus becomes concrete. The Flevoland regioplan already describes a regional operating style based on Positieve Gezondheid, and it explicitly notes that in Positief Gezond Almere the municipality, Elaa, and more than ten city-level care, welfare, and resident organizations are already working together from that perspective. The same regioplan also points to a real implementation vulnerability: the provincial financing for WEL in Flevoland was expected to stop soon, even though the network played a role in knowledge sharing and practical uptake of positive health. citeturn6view1turn6view2

The Positief Gezond Almere transformation plan then translates the high-level D5/D6 logic into executable items. It names, among other things, welzijn op recept, leefstijlloketten, voorzorgcirkels, wijktours, stronger cooperation between informal care, the social domain and the medical domain, data infrastructure, a regional transfer point, monitoring at home, citizen participation, and learning-and-transformation teams inside participating organizations. These are exactly the kinds of practical building blocks that make the accords real at neighborhood and city scale. citeturn20view0

Almere’s own recent political documents broadly support that direction. The coalition agreement continues the positive-health line, explicitly wants stronger ties between the social and medical domains, and says a new municipal health policy note should be brought to council within a year. But the city’s 2026 budget also shows tight fiscal conditions: the budget was balanced only with buffer use, it leaves limited room for one-off extras, and it includes savings on Wmo household support. The implication is not that Almere cannot move; it is that Almere should prioritize low-regret organizational steps first and avoid taking on open-ended structural commitments before the regional approval and funding route is nailed down. That last point is an inference from the documents, not a direct quote. citeturn18search2turn19view0

The regional geography also needs care. A useful external check is the national overview of mental-health networks, which lists Almere as mandaatgemeente for the Flevoland network with Dronten, Lelystad, Noordoostpolder, and Urk, while Zeewolde appears in the separate Gezond Veluwe route. For policymakers, the lesson is that “Flevoland” is not one fixed governance map across all workstreams. Regionality has to be named precisely per dossier. citeturn29view7

## Open questions and practical implications

The main open questions are not about strategic direction; they are about settlement. The repository still treats parts of local teams, digital/operational infrastructure, regional coordination, finance-local alignment, and mental-health-network interpretation as review items. The exact underlying municipal texts for the health vision and the Maatschappelijke Agenda also need to be pulled in more directly. Those gaps do not block analysis, but they do mean some governance and sequencing conclusions should still be treated as working conclusions rather than final settled fact. citeturn14view4turn1view0turn28search20turn28search1

The practical implications for Almere are therefore fairly clear. First, separate formal approval roles from delivery roles, especially where regional social-domain commitments create structural cost. Second, use 2026 to translate existing local practice into the regional AZWA formats rather than building a parallel city-only architecture. Third, align every structural local step with the real funding path: Broad SPUK and IZA accountability rules on one side, breakthrough and transformation windows on the other. Fourth, update the curated source corpus with the actual municipal health-vision and Maatschappelijke Agenda documents, plus the February 2025 local status brief, so that future deep-research work does not depend on summary pages where primary council documents exist. citeturn23view0turn20view0turn19view0turn29view4turn1view0turn26search16
